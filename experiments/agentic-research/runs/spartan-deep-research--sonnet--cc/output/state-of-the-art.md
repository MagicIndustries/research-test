# Agentic Deep Research: State of the Art (mid-2026)

*Compiled 2026-07-18. This is the main document for the report — see [README.md](./README.md) for the index and navigation.*

## TL;DR

Automated "deep research" — an LLM agent that plans, searches, reads, and writes a cited report with minimal supervision — went from a research-lab curiosity (WebGPT, 2021) to a mainstream product category in about four years, with the current wave dating to Google's Gemini Deep Research (Dec 2024) and OpenAI's Deep Research (Feb 2025). Four viable approaches coexist today: single-prompt/skill-based agents (cheap, fast, transparent, good for narrow tasks), orchestrator-worker multi-agent pipelines (best raw quality on broad, parallelizable questions, at ~15x the token cost of a single chat turn per Anthropic's own published figures), hosted commercial products (OpenAI/Google/Perplexity — highest polish, least visibility into method, rate-limited), and open-source frameworks (STORM, GPT Researcher, LangChain/LangGraph "open deep research," and others — fully inspectable and model-agnostic, but uneven maintenance). No approach reliably solves citation faithfulness: even 2026 studies report double-digit hallucinated or fabricated citation rates across every category, commercial and open alike. The evaluation field has converged on rubric-based LLM-as-judge protocols (DeepResearch Bench's RACE/FACT, ResearchRubrics) rather than single-score benchmarks, because "good research report" is inherently multi-dimensional and judged compliance rates on the best current systems still sit under ~68%. For a solo developer working in Claude Code with a skill-comparison harness, the pragmatic path is not to pick one approach but to implement the orchestrator-worker *pattern* using Claude Code's own subagent/Task primitives, benchmark it against a single-prompt skill baseline and 1-2 open-source frameworks inside the existing harness, and treat commercial products as a quality ceiling to compare against rather than a component to build on.

## 1. Definitions

**Deep research** — a class of LLM-agent system that, given an open-ended question, autonomously plans a multi-step investigation, iteratively searches and reads external sources, and synthesizes the results into a structured, citation-backed report, as opposed to answering from a single retrieval pass or from parametric memory. The term itself is a product name (Google and OpenAI both shipped features called "Deep Research" within about ten weeks of each other, Dec 2024 / Feb 2025) that became the generic label for the category. Academic surveys converge on a similar definition: systems that "iteratively integrat[e] planning, retrieval, and synthesis of gathered information with research plans" rather than "providing a single response" ([A Comprehensive Survey of Deep Research](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.12594), Xu & Peng, arXiv:2506.12594, submitted 2025-06-14 — primary, academic). A related survey characterizes deep research agents as combining "dynamic reasoning, adaptive long-horizon planning, multi-hop information retrieval, iterative tool use, and the generation of structured analytical reports" ([Deep Research Agents: A Systematic Examination and Roadmap](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.18096), Huang et al., arXiv:2506.18096, submitted 2025-06-22, revised 2025-09-03 — primary, academic).

**Research agent** — the operational unit inside a deep-research system: an LLM given a goal, a set of tools (web search, page fetch, code execution, file/memory access), and a loop that lets it decide its own next action rather than following a fixed script. A deep-research *system* may consist of one research agent (single-prompt approach) or many coordinated research agents (multi-agent approach).

**Orchestrator–worker** (also "lead agent / subagent" or "orchestrator-subagent") — a multi-agent design pattern where one agent (the orchestrator) decomposes a task and delegates independent pieces to other agents (workers), each running in its own context window with its own tools, before the orchestrator reconciles their outputs. This is Anthropic's own terminology for the architecture behind Claude's Research feature: "a lead agent that coordinates the process while delegating to specialized subagents that operate in parallel" ([How we built our multi-agent research system](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system), Anthropic Engineering, published 2025-06-13 — primary source, but note this is vendor self-reporting about Anthropic's own product and its own internal eval numbers).

**LLM-as-judge** — using a strong LLM, rather than a human or a fixed-answer scorer, to evaluate the quality of another model's (or agent's) open-ended output, typically via pairwise comparison or rubric scoring. Introduced formally in [Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685) (Zheng et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2306.05685 — primary, academic), which found GPT-4-as-judge reached >80% agreement with human preference, matching human-human agreement, while also documenting position bias, verbosity bias, and self-enhancement bias as open problems. This is now the dominant technique for scoring deep-research reports, because there is no single ground-truth "correct" report to grade against (see §5, Evaluation).

## 2. Background: where this came from

The lineage runs: **retrieval-augmented QA → tool-using single agents → deep research agents.**

- **2021 — WebGPT** (OpenAI): an LLM fine-tuned to issue real Bing queries and cite retrieved passages, the first widely cited example of an LLM autonomously browsing to answer a question. Treated as the earliest direct precursor in most surveys.
- **2022–2023 — ReAct** (Yao et al., "ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models," arXiv:2210.03629): interleaving reasoning traces ("thoughts") with actions and environment observations. This pattern became the template that AutoGPT, BabyAGI, LangChain, and essentially every subsequent agent SDK built on. [secondary-sourced synthesis of ReAct's role; primary is the arXiv paper itself]
- **March 2023 — AutoGPT and BabyAGI**: the first widely-demonstrated end-to-end "autonomous" LLM agents wrapping ReAct-style loops with self-directed task lists, popularizing (and over-promising on) fully autonomous agent behavior.
- **December 2024 — Google ships Gemini Deep Research**: the first mainstream product explicitly branded "Deep Research," inside Gemini 2.0/Gemini Advanced, generating a multi-step research plan the user approves before the agent executes it ([Google introduces Gemini 2.0](https://blog.google/technology/google-deepmind/google-gemini-ai-update-december-2024/), 2024-12-11 — primary, vendor).
- **February 2025 — OpenAI ships Deep Research** in ChatGPT, built on an optimized o3 variant, initially Pro-only at $200/mo with a 100-query cap ([Introducing deep research](https://openai.com/index/introducing-deep-research/), OpenAI, 2025-02-02 — primary, vendor). Ten days later, **Perplexity ships its own Deep Research** ([Introducing Perplexity Deep Research](https://www.perplexity.ai/hub/blog/introducing-perplexity-deep-research), 2025-02-14 — primary, vendor), reporting 21.1% on Humanity's Last Exam, ahead of several other model-only baselines at the time (vendor-reported benchmark number — flagged).
- **April–June 2025 — Anthropic ships Claude Research and publishes its architecture**: agentic search with orchestrator-subagent parallelism, and a widely-read engineering post detailing the design trade-offs, becoming a reference architecture cited across the open-source ecosystem ([How we built our multi-agent research system](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system), 2025-06-13 — primary, vendor).
- **2025–2026 — the evaluation and open-source ecosystem catches up**: academic benchmarks purpose-built for deep research (DeepResearch Bench, ResearchRubrics, DeepResearch Bench II, ReportBench, DEER) and open frameworks (STORM, GPT Researcher, LangChain's open deep research, Hugging Face's smolagents reimplementation) mature in parallel, alongside a growing literature specifically on citation hallucination in these systems.

By mid-2026 "deep research" is no longer a single product feature; it is a design pattern implementable at four different levels of investment, which is the map in §3.

## 4. What the best systems share (methodological ingredients)

Cutting across all four approaches, the systems judged highest-quality converge on the same handful of ingredients, per the taxonomies in the two major 2025 surveys ([2506.12594](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.12594), [2506.18096](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.18096) — both primary/academic) and corroborated by Anthropic's engineering write-up:

1. **Explicit planning before searching.** Every mature system generates a research plan or outline first (Gemini Deep Research surfaces this plan for user approval; Anthropic's lead agent "saves its plan to memory" before spawning workers; STORM generates an outline via simulated multi-perspective conversations before writing). Planning is treated as a distinct, inspectable step, not folded into a single opaque prompt.
2. **Iterative search-read-reflect loops, not one-shot retrieval.** Systems re-query based on what they've already found, closing the loop between reading and further searching (the ReAct pattern, still structurally present in 2026 systems).
3. **Parallel decomposition where the task allows it.** Breadth-first questions get fanned out to independent workers/subagents/tool calls running concurrently; Anthropic's own ablation found token usage (i.e., effectively how much parallel work is thrown at the problem) explains ~80% of performance variance on their BrowseComp eval, with tool choice and model choice accounting for the rest — a genuinely load-bearing, if self-reported, empirical claim about *why* the multi-agent approach wins on breadth tasks.
4. **A separate synthesis/citation pass**, distinct from the search agents. Anthropic explicitly uses a separate step to reconcile subagent findings and attach citations; STORM separates outline generation from section writing; most frameworks treat "write the report" as its own stage with its own prompt, not something interleaved with searching.
5. **Structured output scaffolding** (headings, per-section citation requirements, rubric-like internal checklists) rather than free-form generation — this shows up both in product design (all three commercial products produce report-shaped output with inline citations) and in the evaluation literature, where rubric/checklist-based judging (§5) works far better than holistic scoring, suggesting the generation side benefits from the same structure the judging side needs.
6. **Source diversity and cross-checking as an explicit step**, not an assumed byproduct of "search a lot" — the strongest systems verify claims against multiple sources rather than trusting single-source retrieval, though per §5 this remains the single weakest point in practice across the entire field.

What is *not* shared, and remains genuinely unresolved: how to guarantee citation faithfulness. This is covered next.

## 5. What the evaluation literature says

Because there is no single correct "deep research report" for any open-ended question, the field cannot use exact-match or reference-answer scoring the way narrower NLP tasks can. Two things have emerged to fill the gap:

**Rubric-based LLM-as-judge protocols.** [DeepResearch Bench II](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.08536) (arXiv:2601.08536, submitted 2026-02-02 — primary, academic) and its predecessor evaluate 100 (then 132) PhD-level tasks across 22 expert-curated domains, calibrated against ~96,000 real user queries, using two complementary judges: **RACE**, an LLM-judge protocol scoring reports against a reference along adaptively-weighted dimensions (comprehensiveness, insight, instruction-following, readability), and **FACT**, which specifically verifies citation/claim pairs for citation accuracy. [ResearchRubrics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.07685) (Sharma, Zhang et al., arXiv:2511.07685, submitted 2025-11-10 — primary, academic) goes further, pairing realistic prompts with 2,500+ expert-written fine-grained rubrics assessing factual grounding, reasoning soundness, and clarity, built with over 2,800 hours of human labor; it reports that **leading systems achieve under 68% rubric compliance**, with failures concentrated in "missed implicit context and inadequate reasoning about retrieved information" rather than raw retrieval failure. This is a load-bearing, primary, non-vendor finding: even the best deep-research systems in late 2025 were failing roughly a third of expert-defined quality checks.

**Citation and hallucination auditing as a separate axis from "quality."** A cluster of 2025-2026 papers treats citation faithfulness as its own measurable failure mode, independent of how good the prose reads:
- [Cited but Not Verified: Parsing and Evaluating Source Attribution in LLM Deep Research Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06635) (primary, academic).
- [Why Your Deep Research Agent Fails? On Hallucination Evaluation in Full Research Trajectory](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.22984) (primary, academic) argues most evaluation is outcome-oriented and fails to trace hallucinations back to the specific intermediate claim or action that produced them, distinguishing *statement hallucination* (content deviates from the cited source) from *citation hallucination* (the reference itself is fabricated).
- Reported numbers vary widely by study and methodology and should be treated as bracketing estimates, not a single consensus figure: one line of work finds 3-13% of URLs fabricated even in retrieval-augmented settings; a separate concurrent benchmark ("GhostCite," per secondary reporting) reports hallucination rates from 14% up to 95% across 13 LLMs and 40 domains, which is a very wide spread and a sign the field has not agreed on a measurement standard — flagged as an open question, not a settled number.
- On mitigation, extended/deliberate reasoning appears to help substantially: one report cites GPT-5.5 Pro's hallucination rate dropping from 8.3% to 4.2%, and Claude Opus 4.7's from 9.4% to 5.1%, when extended thinking is enabled — but this figure comes from secondary reporting aggregating vendor and third-party numbers, not a single peer-reviewed primary source, and should be treated cautiously.

**Net assessment:** the evaluation literature has matured from "does it sound good" (informal, vendor-driven comparisons in 2025 blog posts) to structured, rubric- and citation-audited benchmarks by 2026, and the consistent finding across independent (non-vendor) benchmarks is that no current system — commercial or open — reliably clears even a 70% bar on expert-defined correctness/faithfulness rubrics. Anyone building or comparing a research skill should adopt rubric + citation-audit scoring rather than a single holistic "was this good" LLM judge call, and should not trust any single hallucination-rate number without checking the measurement methodology behind it.

## 6. What we could not find

- **No single authoritative, continuously-updated leaderboard** across all four approach categories (single-prompt, multi-agent, commercial, open-source) evaluated on identical tasks with identical judging. DeepResearch Bench and ResearchRubrics each evaluate a subset of systems, not all of them, and commercial products are evaluated inconsistently (sometimes via API, sometimes via UI scraping) which limits comparability.
- **No vendor (OpenAI, Google, Perplexity, Anthropic) publishes its full system prompt, tool schema, or orchestration logic** for its commercial product — all trade-off analysis of "how it works" for commercial products in this report is necessarily reconstructed from disclosed architecture notes, third-party probing, and vendor blog claims, not verified source. This is flagged explicitly in the commercial-products section.
- **No consensus hallucination-rate figure.** As noted in §5, published fabricated-citation rates for deep research agents range from single digits to over 90% depending on study, domain, and definition of "hallucination." We could not find a reconciliation of this spread.
- **Limited independent, apples-to-apples cost data.** Vendor pricing is public, but real per-report cost (tokens actually consumed, search API calls, retries) for a given quality bar is mostly self-reported or anecdotal; we could not find a rigorous third-party cost/quality Pareto study across approaches.
- Any additional specific gaps are called out inline within each subdirectory's README and resources file, per the "I couldn't find data on X" rule.

## 7. Recommended steps for a solo developer working in Claude Code with a skill-comparison harness

This section assumes: you already have a git-repo-based harness for comparing research skills/prompts, you work in Claude Code daily, and your goal is to assemble the best achievable research capability from existing parts rather than build something novel from scratch.

1. **Establish your evaluation contract before improving the skill.** Bolt DeepResearch-Bench-style rubric scoring (comprehensiveness, insight, instruction-following, readability) plus a dedicated citation-audit pass (does every load-bearing claim in a report trace to a real, fetched, on-topic source) onto your harness first. Without this, comparisons between skill variants are vibes-based. This is the single highest-leverage step per §5: current best systems fail ~1/3 of expert rubrics, so your harness needs to be able to see that kind of failure, not just judge "sounds comprehensive."
2. **Keep a single-prompt skill (like the one this report was produced under) as your cheap baseline**, not your ceiling. It's the right tool for narrow, depth-first questions and for fast harness iteration because each run is one auditable trace at low token cost — but treat it as a floor to beat, since the evidence in §3/§4 is that breadth-first tasks specifically benefit from parallel decomposition.
3. **Build an orchestrator-worker variant using Claude Code's own subagent/Task primitives**, since you already have them natively (`.claude/agents/*.md` definitions, the `Agent`/Task tool for spawning parallel subagents, background execution for long-running research fan-out). This directly mirrors Anthropic's own published reference architecture (lead agent plans and saves plan to memory → spawns parallel workers with independent context/tools → separate synthesis+citation pass) without needing an external orchestration framework like LangGraph. Expect ~15x the token cost of your single-prompt baseline per Anthropic's own figure, and reserve it for genuinely breadth-first tasks in your harness's task set, not every comparison run.
4. **Borrow specific techniques from open-source frameworks rather than adopting a whole framework**, since your harness is already git-repo-native and Claude-Code-first: study STORM's outline-then-write / multi-perspective question generation and GPT Researcher's planner-executor + multi-retriever aggregation (see [approaches/open-source-frameworks/](./approaches/open-source-frameworks/README.md)), and port the specific mechanic (e.g., perspective-based query generation, or a dedicated citation-verification agent step) into your own skill/subagent definitions rather than depending on an external framework's runtime.
5. **Use commercial products (OpenAI Deep Research, Gemini Deep Research, Perplexity Deep Research) as a quality ceiling in your harness, not a component.** Periodically run the same prompts through one or more of them and score their output with your own rubric+citation harness alongside your own variants — this gives you an external reference point without taking a dependency, and matches how §3 frames their trade-off (highest polish, lowest transparency, not composable into your pipeline).
6. **Instrument for the two failure modes the evaluation literature actually flags**: rubric non-compliance (missed implicit context, weak reasoning over retrieved material) and citation hallucination (fabricated or mismatched sources) — track them as separate metrics in your harness rather than a single quality score, since they have different root causes and different fixes (planning/reflection quality vs. a verification pass).
7. **Re-run your harness's cross-approach comparison periodically, not once.** This space moved from zero commercial products to four major ones in about ten weeks (Dec 2024-Feb 2025) and both the open-source and evaluation ecosystems are still actively publishing (multiple relevant papers dated January-June 2026 surfaced during this research). Treat this document's approach-map as a mid-2026 snapshot, not a permanent ranking.
