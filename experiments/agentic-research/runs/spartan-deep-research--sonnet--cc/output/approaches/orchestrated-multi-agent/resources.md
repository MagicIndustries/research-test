# Resources: Orchestrated Multi-Agent Research Pipelines

## Repos

- **langchain-ai/open_deep_research** — https://github.com/langchain-ai/open_deep_research — updated through at least August 2025. Open-source, LangGraph-based orchestrator-worker research agent supporting multiple model providers and search tools; ~12,000 GitHub stars, ranked #6 on DeepResearch Bench (RACE 0.4344 default config, 0.4943 with GPT-5) as of an August 2, 2025 update. [primary] (repo/author claims about its own design and benchmark placement; not independently re-verified beyond the leaderboard site).
- **langchain-ai/deep_research_from_scratch** — https://github.com/langchain-ai/deep_research_from_scratch — companion teaching repo that builds an orchestrator-worker research agent incrementally; useful as a minimal reference implementation of the pattern. [primary]
- **langchain-ai/deepagents** — https://github.com/langchain-ai/deepagents — "batteries-included agent harness" including deep-research-style orchestration primitives (planning, sub-agent spawning, virtual filesystem for shared state). [primary]
- **langchain-ai/async-deep-agents** — https://github.com/langchain-ai/async-deep-agents — reference implementation of non-blocking orchestrator/worker execution (supervisor launches workers as background jobs and collects results asynchronously) on LangGraph/LangSmith deployments. [primary]
- **stanford-oval/storm** — https://storm-project.stanford.edu/research/storm/ — Stanford's STORM system; a related but distinct fan-out/fan-in multi-role pattern (simulated multi-perspective interviews) for pre-writing research and outline generation, predating the "orchestrator-worker" terminology. [primary/academic]
- **Ayanami0730/deep_research_bench** — https://github.com/Ayanami0730/deep_research_bench — companion code/data for the DeepResearch Bench paper (below); used to benchmark deep-research agents including multi-agent ones. [primary]

## Papers

- **"How we built our multi-agent research system"** — Anthropic Engineering, June 13, 2025 — https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system — [primary] The key primary source for this whole slice. Describes the LeadResearcher/subagent/CitationAgent orchestrator-worker architecture behind Claude's Research feature, prompt-engineering lessons, failure modes, and evaluation methodology. Contains vendor self-reported performance numbers (90.2% improvement over single-agent Opus 4 on an internal breadth-first eval; ~15x token cost vs. chat; token usage explains ~80% of BrowseComp variance) — flagged as vendor self-report, not independently replicated.
- **"DeepResearch Bench: A Comprehensive Benchmark for Deep Research Agents"** — Du, Xu, Zhu, Wang, Mao — arXiv:2506.11763, June 16, 2025 — https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.11763 — [primary/academic] Introduces the RACE (report quality) and FACT (citation/retrieval accuracy) evaluation frameworks and a 100-task, 22-field benchmark used to rank both single- and multi-agent research systems, including `open_deep_research`. Independent of Anthropic.
- **"Towards a science of scaling agent systems: When and why agent systems work"** — Kim & Liu, Google Research, Jan 28, 2026 — https://research.google/blog/towards-a-science-of-scaling-agent-systems-when-and-why-agent-systems-work/ (paper: arXiv:2512.08296) — [secondary, independent academic] Quantifies when multi-agent beats single-agent: +81% on a parallelizable financial-reasoning benchmark, but –39% to –70% on a sequential planning benchmark (PlanCraft); reports centralized/orchestrated coordination contains error amplification to ~4.4x versus ~17.2x for uncoordinated multi-agent systems. Strong independent cross-check on Anthropic's "breadth-first wins, sequential loses" claim, from a different lab.
- **"Auto Research with Specialist Agents Develops Effective and Non-Trivial Training Recipes"** — arXiv:2605.05724 — https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.05724 — [primary/academic] Multi-specialist-agent research system applied to ML training-recipe discovery; surfaced in search as adjacent evidence of orchestrator/specialist-agent patterns being applied beyond web research. Not deeply verified in this pass — flagged for follow-up if used as a load-bearing claim.
- **"Detecting and Correcting Reference Hallucinations in Commercial LLMs and Deep Research Agents"** — arXiv:2604.03173 — https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.03173 — [secondary/academic] Relevant to the citation-agent design question (why Anthropic separates citation-mapping into its own pass); not fetched in full for this pass, flagged for follow-up.

## Articles

- **"Anthropic: How we built our multi-agent research system"** — Simon Willison, June 14, 2025 — https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/14/multi-agent-research-system/ — [secondary] Widely-cited independent commentary/summary from a well-known LLM practitioner; confirms and restates the 90.2% and 15x figures (both traced back to Anthropic, not independently measured) and highlights the "tool-testing agent cut completion time 40%" detail.
- **"How and when to build multi-agent systems"** — LangChain blog, June 16, 2025 — https://www.langchain.com/blog/how-and-when-to-build-multi-agent-systems — [secondary] Practitioner guidance converging with Anthropic's post: multi-agent favors parallelizable "read-heavy" breadth-first tasks; single agent favors "write-heavy"/tightly-coupled tasks; names context misalignment and conflicting outputs as the core coordination failure modes.
- **"Don't Build Multi-Agents"** — Walden Yan, Cognition AI, 2025 — https://cognition.com/blog/dont-build-multi-agents — [secondary, vendor self-report from a competing agent-tooling company] The most-cited counterpoint to the orchestrator-worker pattern: argues sub-agents lacking shared intermediate context produce conflicting outputs ("actions carry implicit decisions, and conflicting decisions carry bad results"), illustrated with a two-agent Flappy-Bird build that produces visually incoherent output. Argues for single-threaded linear agents instead — note this critique is aimed primarily at coding/write-heavy tasks, not read-heavy research, and Anthropic's own post implicitly agrees with the boundary.
- **"How OpenAI, Gemini, and Claude Use Agents to Power Deep Research"** — ByteByteGo blog — https://blog.bytebytego.com/p/how-openai-gemini-and-claude-use — [secondary, unverified against vendors' own docs] Characterizes OpenAI's and Google's hosted "Deep Research" products as closer to a single strong reasoning-model agent rather than an explicit multi-agent orchestrator, in contrast to Anthropic's publicly-documented multi-agent design. Useful for framing but should be treated as an outside characterization, not confirmed by OpenAI/Google primary sources.
- **Deep Research Bench Leaderboard** — FutureSearch — https://futuresearch.ai/deep-research-bench/ — [secondary, third-party evaluator] Ongoing leaderboard tracking commercial and open-source deep-research agents (Gemini Deep Research, OpenAI Deep Research, `open_deep_research`, etc.); as of the pass reflected here, Gemini-2.5-Pro Deep Research led overall (48.88) with OpenAI Deep Research close behind (46.98); notes a June 17, 2026 evaluator-model change (switched to GPT-5.5) following Gemini-2.5-Pro's deprecation — flag this as a moving target, not a fixed number.
- **"The Art of Multi-Agent Collaboration: Deep Reflections on Anthropic's Research System Engineering"** — https://llmmultiagents.com/en/blogs/anthropic-multi-agent-system-reflection — [secondary] Extended independent commentary/reflection on the Anthropic post; not independently fact-checked in this pass beyond confirming it restates the primary source.

## Notes on cross-checking and gaps

- The 90.2% performance-improvement figure and the ~15x token-cost multiplier both trace to a single Anthropic source; every secondary article found (Willison, LangChain, ByteByteGo, Medium summaries) repeats the same numbers rather than independently re-measuring them. Treat as one vendor-reported data point, not a cross-validated consensus.
- The Google Research (Kim & Liu, Jan 2026) paper is the strongest independent, non-Anthropic quantitative evidence found for the general "multi-agent wins on parallelizable tasks, loses on sequential tasks" claim, using different benchmarks (Finance-Agent, PlanCraft) than Anthropic's BrowseComp-based eval — directionally consistent, not numerically comparable.
- Adoption statistics circulating in generic 2026 "enterprise AI orchestration" trade-blog content (e.g., claims of "340% YoY enterprise adoption growth" or "73% of Fortune 500 deploying multi-agent workflows") were found during this research pass but are not included above — they came from SEO-oriented secondary sites without clear primary sourcing or methodology and could not be corroborated; flagged here as likely unreliable rather than cited as fact.
- No independent (non-Anthropic) production-scale case study with its own published token-cost or quality numbers for an orchestrator-worker *research* pipeline specifically (as opposed to general multi-agent enterprise workflows) was found in this pass; this is a genuine gap rather than an oversight.
